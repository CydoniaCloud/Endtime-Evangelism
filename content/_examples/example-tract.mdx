The Bible never says the fruit was an apple. The Genesis text simply calls
it the fruit of "the tree of the knowledge of good and evil," and Hebrew
gives no botanical clue. The apple entered the picture much later — through
medieval European art and a Latin pun on _malum_, which means both "apple"
and "evil."

Why does the detail matter? Because the question Genesis 3 actually asks
is not _which fruit_ but _why this story is the doorway every life walks
through_. The fall is not a fairy tale about produce. It is the moment the
human heart first preferred its own counsel to God's, and every conscience
since has felt the echo.

This piece walks slowly through the text — what the words say, what they do
not, and what the rest of Scripture says the cure has always been.

## What the text says

In Genesis 2:9, the tree is named for what it produces in the one who
takes from it: the knowledge of good and evil. Until that moment, Adam
and Eve knew good as the air they breathed. They did not need to weigh
it against its opposite, because they did not know its opposite.

> Of every tree of the garden thou mayest freely eat: but of the tree of
> the knowledge of good and evil, thou shalt not eat of it.

The prohibition is not arbitrary. The tree is a kind of mirror. To take
from it is to demand the right to judge for oneself what is good and
what is not — to be, in that small but seismic way, one's own god.

## What is not in the text

The word _apple_ never appears. Neither does any image of orchards or
crisp red fruit. The serpent does not say "eat this apple." Eve does not
hand Adam an apple. The fruit's species is not the point.

It became "an apple" in the West largely through the Vulgate, the Latin
translation completed by Jerome around AD 400. The word for "evil" in
Latin is _malum_; the word for "apple" is _mālum_. Same spelling, slightly
different vowel length. Medieval readers made the link, painters followed,
and a thousand years later we still picture an apple.

## What this means for us

If the fruit was not the point, what was? Genesis tells us: a will turned
in on itself, a trust withdrawn, a relationship broken. Whatever variety
the tree was, the wound it opened is the one every human carries. We all
know good and evil now, and we all keep choosing the second more often
than we mean to.

The Bible's answer to this fall is not a better fruit but a better Adam.
Where the first man chose self, the second man — Jesus — chose obedience
all the way down to a cross. What was lost in a garden is being given back
in a garden where the tomb stood empty on a Sunday morning.

If that has stirred something in you, read on.
