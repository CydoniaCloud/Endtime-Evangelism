If you have ever stood at a graveside and thought, _is this all?_ — you
have asked the question every human eventually asks. The Bible answers
it directly. Whether you come to that answer reluctantly or hopefully,
this essay walks slowly through what Scripture says about the hour after
the last breath.

## The hour that is coming

Jesus put the answer in a single sentence:

<PullQuote scriptureRef="john-5" verse="28-29">
  Marvel not at this: for the hour is coming, in the which all that are
  in the graves shall hear his voice, and shall come forth; they that
  have done good, unto the resurrection of life; and they that have done
  evil, unto the resurrection of damnation.
</PullQuote>

Two resurrections. One leads into life. The other into judgment. The
hour is coming for everyone — Christian or otherwise — and on that hour
Scripture speaks plainly.

## The longer hope

Long before the New Testament was written, the prophet Job was already
asking the question. He watched a tree cut down by an axe and saw it
spring back when the rains came, and he wondered:

<PullQuote scriptureRef="job-14" verse="14">
  If a man die, shall he live again? All the days of my appointed time
  will I wait, till my change come.
</PullQuote>

Later, in the same book, the answer arrived as conviction:

<PullQuote scriptureRef="job-19" verse="25-26">
  For I know that my redeemer liveth, and that he shall stand at the
  latter day upon the earth: and though after my skin worms destroy this
  body, yet in my flesh shall I see God.
</PullQuote>

Job had no Bible to read. He simply knew. The shape of the Christian
hope — that the body broken in death will be raised by the Redeemer — is
older than any of the books that bear witness to it.

## Heaven and hell

Just as Scripture does not pretend death is small, it does not pretend
that what comes after is uniformly safe. Paul, writing to the
Corinthians, described what is prepared for those who love God:

<PullQuote scriptureRef="1-corinthians-2" verse="9">
  Eye hath not seen, nor ear heard, neither have entered into the heart
  of man, the things which God hath prepared for them that love him.
</PullQuote>

Heaven is greater than imagination can hold.

But Jesus also spoke directly about a different outcome. He did not
soften it for the sake of his hearers. In Mark 9 he said it would be
better to enter life maimed than to keep both hands and go into "the
fire that never shall be quenched." The horror of being eternally cut
off from God is real enough that he warned of it plainly. The stakes of
this life are higher than the surface of it suggests.

## The narrow gate

Then there is the question of who will be on which side of that hour.
Jesus's answer is sobering:

<PullQuote scriptureRef="matthew-7" verse="13-14">
  Enter ye in at the strait gate: for wide is the gate, and broad is the
  way, that leadeth to destruction, and many there be which go in
  thereat: because strait is the gate, and narrow is the way, which
  leadeth unto life, and few there be that find it.
</PullQuote>

A few verses later he says something even more pointed. On the day of
judgment, many will appeal to him by his own name — _Lord, Lord, have we
not prophesied in thy name?_ — and he will reply, _I never knew you;
depart from me, ye that work iniquity_ (Matthew 7:22–23).

The implication is hard. Christianity is not a label you wear. It is a
relationship you actually have. Many who claim the name will not, on the
last day, hear their name claimed back.

## What Jesus asked for

So how is the relationship made? Jesus's own answer is direct:

<PullQuote scriptureRef="john-5" verse="24">
  Verily, verily, I say unto you, He that heareth my word, and believeth
  on him that sent me, hath everlasting life, and shall not come into
  condemnation; but is passed from death unto life.
</PullQuote>

Two verbs: _hear_ and _believe_. Hear his word — which is to say, take
Scripture seriously, attend to what Jesus actually said. Believe on the
Father who sent him. The one who does these things has already passed
out of death, even before the body has stopped breathing.

This is not the moralistic answer you may have heard from somewhere
else. Eternal life is not given to people who try to be good enough. It
is given to people who hear and trust. Goodness — the slow remaking of a
life — follows the trust. It is not the price for it.

## Two sentences, on the day

In Matthew 25, Jesus describes the day of judgment and the two sentences
he will say. To one side:

> Come, ye blessed of my Father, inherit the kingdom prepared for you
> from the foundation of the world.

To the other:

> Depart from me, ye cursed, into everlasting fire, prepared for the
> devil and his angels.

Which sentence will be yours is decided here, in this life, before the
last breath, by what you do with Jesus.

## Where will you spend eternity?

If the question feels urgent, it is. Not in a way that pressures you,
but in the way the question of where you will sleep tonight is urgent
when it begins to rain. The day is coming for everyone. The right
response now is not panic. It is honest attention.

If you have not yet, take time to read what Jesus said about himself —
John's gospel is a good place to begin. Then, if it lands, the next
